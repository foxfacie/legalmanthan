# Anticipatory Bail under the NDPS Act, 1985

The Supreme Court in **Dinesh Chander v. State of Haryana (2025)** reiterated that *anticipatory bail is never granted in NDPS cases*, particularly when commercial quantities of contraband are involved.

---

## Case Overview: Dinesh Chander v. State of Haryana (2025)

- **Court:** Supreme Court of India  
- **Bench:** Justices Pankaj Mithal and KV Viswanathan  
- **Observation:** "Anticipatory bail is never granted in NDPS cases."  

### Background
- The petitioner, Dinesh Chander, was named as a **supplier** of contraband by co-accused persons.  
- Recovery: **60 kgs of Doda Post** and **1.8 kgs of opium** from co-accused.  
- Chander was not in the original FIR but was later implicated based on **disclosure statements**.  
- Case involved **commercial quantity**, triggering the strict provisions of **Section 37 NDPS Act**.  

### Court Proceedings
- **High Court (Punjab & Haryana):** Denied anticipatory bail citing need for custodial interrogation.  
- **Supreme Court:** Upheld the decision, observing no error in refusing bail.  
- **Evidence:** Telephonic conversations and bank transactions between the petitioner and co-accused.  

### Supreme Court’s Observations
- Anticipatory bail is generally **barred in NDPS cases** due to the seriousness of drug offences.  
- The Court suggested the petitioner could **surrender before the Trial Court** and seek **regular bail**.  
- Strict approach is necessary to combat **drug trafficking networks**.  

---

## About the NDPS Act, 1985

The **Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985** regulates and prohibits:  
- Cultivation, production, manufacture, possession, sale, transport, warehousing, import, export, and use of narcotic drugs and psychotropic substances.  
- Quantities classified as **small, intermediate, and commercial**, with varying punishments.  
- Special powers for search and seizure, and a **reverse burden of proof** on the accused in certain cases.  

---

## Section 37 – Special Provisions on Bail

Section 37 makes **grant of bail extremely restrictive** in NDPS cases.  

### Key Points:
- All offences under the Act are **cognizable**.  
- Bail for offences under **Sections 19, 24, 27A** and cases involving **commercial quantity** is highly restricted.  
- Bail cannot be granted unless:  
  1. The Public Prosecutor is given an opportunity to oppose.  
  2. The Court is satisfied that:  
     - The accused is **not guilty** of the offence.  
     - The accused is **not likely to commit further offences** on bail.  

### Section 37(2)
- Limitations on bail under NDPS are **in addition to** those under CrPC or any other law.  
- Applies to **both regular bail and anticipatory bail**, making anticipatory bail almost impossible in NDPS cases.  

---

## Key Takeaways
- **Anticipatory bail is practically barred in NDPS cases**, especially where commercial quantities are involved.  
- The **Supreme Court in 2025** reaffirmed this principle in *Dinesh Chander v. State of Haryana*.  
- Section 37 NDPS Act imposes **stringent conditions** for granting bail, making it more restrictive than ordinary criminal law.
